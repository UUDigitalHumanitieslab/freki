doc_id=16.tetml page=2 block_id=2-4 bbox=94.1,576.9,507.3,754.3 12 26
line=12  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:   Recent work in morphology in LFG (Nordlinger 1998, Sadler 1998, Barron 1998) has highlighted
line=13  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:the fact that NPs in some languages inflect for the traditionally verbal categories of tense, aspect
line=14  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:or mood (henceforth TAM). This phenomenon is extremely problematic for head-driven approaches
line=15  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:such as HPSG, which assumes that clause-level information will be associated with clausal heads,
line=16  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:and not with nominal arguments or adjuncts.  In this paper we show first that the phenomenon of
line=17  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:TAM-inflected nominals is well established and not typologically marginal. We discuss data from a
line=18  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:range of typologically diverse languages and show that such data cannot simply be reanalysed to fit
line=19  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:head-driven approaches, but demand an analysis in which clause-level information such as TAM is directly
line=20  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:      contributed by nominal arguments. We then go on to show how the correspondence architecture
line=21  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:    of LFG, and particularly the constructive morphology approach currently being developed within
line=22  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:it (Nordlinger 1998, Sadler 1998, Barron 1998, Sells 1998, Lee 1999, Sharma 1999) permits a simple
line=23  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:and natural analysis of these data, which are extremely problematic for other formal approaches. This
line=24  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:approach not only provides an explanatory account for the cross-linguistic phenomenon of tense as a
line=25  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:nominal category, but also highlights one of the strengths of LFG in contrast with head-driven frameworks
line=26  tag=O fonts=F2-9.96:      .
